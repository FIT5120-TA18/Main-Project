What is housing stress?

Housing stress means you are spending more than 30 cents of every dollar you earn on rent.

The Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) uses the 30% threshold as a standard measure of rental affordability. If your rent takes up more than 30% of your income, you have less money left over for other essential expenses like food, transport, healthcare, and savings.

Why it matters

  • Less money for basics: You might skip meals, go without transport, or delay healthcare to cover rent.
  • No emergency buffer: One unexpected expense (car repair, medical bill) can push you into debt.
  • Harder to save: You can't build savings or invest in your future (education, career training).
  • Stress and mental health: Financial pressure affects your wellbeing and can limit your choices.

What you can do

  • Share housing: Shared rentals are cheaper per person than living alone.
  • Move to a lower-cost area: Some suburbs or regions have cheaper rents. Consider transport costs too.
  • Increase your income: Work more hours, upskill, or negotiate a raise to improve your ratio.
  • Seek support: Talk to a community centre, financial counsellor, or local housing service about assistance programs.
Remember: Housing stress is common for young people, but it's not permanent. As you earn more and find stable housing, your situation will improve.

Source: ABS Housing Occupancy and Costs 20.2019
