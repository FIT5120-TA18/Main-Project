Credit & BNPL 101

Explore ↓

Understanding credit cards and buy-now-pay-later services is essential to avoiding hidden debt traps. Here's what you need to know.

credit_card How Credit Cards Work

A credit card lets you spend money you don't yet have, up to a set limit. If you pay the full balance each month before the due date, you pay no interest. If you pay only the minimum, the remaining balance accrues interest at around 20% per year — meaning a $500 balance left unpaid costs you ~$100 extra over 12 months.

Late payment fees are charged when you miss a payment. These compound quickly if your balance keeps growing.

shopping_bag BNPL (Afterpay, Zip & more)

BNPL lets you split a purchase into 4 fortnightly payments at no interest — but miss a payment and you're charged a late fee (Afterpay charges $10 per missed instalment). Multiple active BNPL schedules can create a cascade of fees if a bad week hits your income.

BNPL activity is increasingly being reported to credit bureaus, meaning missed payments can affect your credit score.

warning Hidden Conditions to Watch

  • Interest-free periods end — often without warning
  • Minimum repayments only reduce your balance slowly
  • Store credit cards often have higher rates (25–30% p.a.)
  • BNPL is not always free — some providers charge account fees

Tax & Super Basics

Explore ↓

When you start working, understanding your payslip, how tax works, and what superannuation is will save you stress and money.

description Your Payslip Explained

A sample payslip for someone earning $750/week gross:

Sample Payslip — Weekly
Gross Income Before tax $750.00
Tax Withheld (PAYG) Deduction −$97.00
Superannuation (11.5%) Your future $86.25
Net (Take-Home) Pay $653.00

Super is paid by your employer in addition to your wage — it goes into your super fund, not your bank account.

assignment_return Tax Return Tips

Each year, the ATO calculates whether you've paid too much or too little tax. If you've overpaid, you receive a refund. Common deductions for young workers:

  • Work-related clothing (uniforms, protective gear)
  • Home office expenses (if you WFH)
  • Study costs directly related to your current job
  • Union fees and professional memberships
  • Work-related phone and internet use

savings Understanding Superannuation

Super is money set aside for your retirement. Your employer must contribute 11.5% of your wages (from July 2024) into your chosen super fund. Over a working lifetime, this grows significantly through compounding interest.

Always check you have only one super fund — multiple funds mean multiple sets of fees eating into your balance.

Smart Budgeting Strategies

Explore ↓

Budgeting doesn't have to be complicated. Here are three simple methods — pick whichever fits your lifestyle.

🥊 The 50/30/20 Rule

Split your after-tax income: 50% on needs (rent, groceries, transport), 30% on wants (dining out, entertainment), 20% on savings and debt repayment. Simple and flexible for irregular incomes.

💸 Pay Yourself First

As soon as you get paid, transfer a set amount to savings before spending anything else. Even $20/week adds up to $1,040 a year. Automate it so you don't have to think about it.

✉️ The Envelope Method

Allocate cash (or separate bank accounts) for each spending category. Once an envelope is empty, you stop spending in that category. Works great for controlling discretionary spending.

lightbulb Quick Wins

  • Review your subscriptions monthly — cancel what you don't use
  • Cook in bulk to reduce food spend by up to 40%
  • Use public transport concession cards if eligible
  • Set a weekly "fun money" limit so you don't overspend

Victorian Tenant Rights

Explore ↓

Renting for the first time? Understanding your rights as a tenant in Victoria protects you from unfair treatment and unexpected costs.

gavel Key Rights Before You Sign

  • You must receive a copy of the rental agreement before signing
  • Bond cannot exceed one month's rent for rentals under $900/week
  • Landlords must provide a condition report within 3 business days of moving in
  • Rent increases must be in writing with at least 60 days notice
  • Landlords cannot increase rent more than once every 12 months

construction Repairs & Maintenance

Your landlord is responsible for keeping the property in good repair. Urgent repairs (broken heater, no hot water, security issues) must be fixed promptly. If they refuse, you can apply to VCAT for orders.

attach_money Getting Your Bond Back

Bond is held by the Residential Tenancies Bond Authority (RTBA). At the end of your tenancy, the landlord can only claim bond for genuine damage beyond normal wear and tear. Take photos on move-in day!
